Overview
Executive Summary

Jethur is a well-positioned GRC SaaS platform with strong regional expertise across KSA, MENA, APAC, and Europe, and meaningful credibility signals — most notably the RegTech100 2026 recognition. Despite the strength of the product, the current website significantly underperforms as a sales and marketing asset.

This audit documented 30 issues across 7 categories. The most critical are active conversion failures: the header disappears on scroll, the only call-to-action commits visitors to an immediate demo booking, there is no pricing information, no case studies, and no visual demonstration of the product anywhere. For enterprise buyers evaluating GRC software, these are disqualifying gaps.

Additional problems exist at the technical level — the homepage returns a 403 Forbidden error to direct HTTP requests, meaning search engine crawlers may not be able to index the site. Privacy Policy and Terms & Conditions links in the footer are broken, pointing to "#". These are not UX issues — they are legal and credibility risks that active prospects will encounter.

Issue Findings
Website Issues — 30 Findings
1
Header Is Not Sticky — Primary CTA Disappears on Scroll
The site header does not remain fixed as users scroll. On long pages — the homepage, product pages, and /jethur-ai — the "Schedule Demo" button vanishes entirely once the user scrolls past the fold. Enterprise buyers often spend several minutes reading content before acting; by that point the only CTA on the page is gone.
2
Navigation Does Not Surface Pricing or Case Studies
The current navigation structure has no direct link to a pricing page or a case studies/resources section — both of which enterprise buyers navigate to immediately. These pages don't exist on the current site, but their absence from navigation also means there is no expectation set that they might exist elsewhere.
3
Mega Menu Is Plain Text — No Visual Engagement
The Standards & Regulations dropdown is a bare two-column list of text links with no icons, no descriptions, no hover treatments, and no visual hierarchy beyond the column headers. Enterprise buyers scanning this menu have no context for what each standard means to their business. Competitors use mega menus with icons, short descriptors, and featured content to guide buyers — the current menu adds no persuasive value and is unlikely to drive clicks.
4
Only One CTA Exists Across the Entire Site
Every CTA on the site — on every page — directs the visitor to "Schedule Demo". There is no alternative: no content download, no product overview, no pricing inquiry, no newsletter. Visitors who are not yet ready to commit to a meeting — which is the majority on a first visit — have no engagement option and leave without any contact being made.
5
No CTAs Within Long-Form Page Content
Product pages, solution pages, and the Standards & Regulations section contain long-form content with no in-body CTAs. A user who reads to the bottom of the ISO 27001 page, for example, has demonstrated intent — but encounters no next step. The page simply ends.
6
CTA Button Styling Is Inconsistent Across Pages
Primary CTA buttons vary in colour, size, and visual treatment between pages. There is no consistent component — the button on the homepage looks different from the one on /jethur-ai and different again on solution pages. This inconsistency signals a lack of systematic design and can undermine perceived professionalism for buyers scrutinising the site carefully.
7
No Re-engagement Mechanism for Exiting Visitors
There is no exit-intent mechanism, no "save for later" option, and no confirmed remarketing pixel active on the site. Visitors who leave — especially those who browsed multiple pages — are lost entirely with no path to re-engagement.
8
No Pricing Page Exists
There is no pricing page, pricing section, or any pricing signal anywhere on the current site. Enterprise GRC buyers consistently cite pricing transparency as a top evaluation factor — even a "contact us for pricing" page communicates that pricing exists and is obtainable. The complete absence forces buyers to commit to a sales conversation before they can assess whether Jethur is in their budget, which most will not do.
9
No Pricing Model Communicated Anywhere
There is no indication of how Jethur is priced — not by module, by seat, by organisation size, or as an enterprise contract. This is distinct from having no pricing page: the pricing model itself is entirely invisible. Buyers cannot even begin to estimate commercial fit.
10
No Self-Serve or Low-Friction Entry Point
Competing platforms including Vanta and LogicGate offer free trials, interactive product tours, or compliance readiness assessments as entry points that don't require scheduling. Jethur's only entry point is a calendar booking, which is the highest-friction option available and filters out a significant portion of evaluators at the awareness stage.
11
Privacy Policy and Terms & Conditions Links Are Broken
Both the Privacy Policy and Terms & Conditions links in the footer point to "#" — they navigate nowhere. This is a legal risk under GDPR and PDPL, and a direct trust failure. Enterprise procurement and legal teams check these pages as a standard step in vendor evaluation. A GRC platform with broken legal links is immediately disqualifying for buyers in regulated industries.
12
No Case Studies or Client Success Evidence
There are no case studies, client testimonials with outcomes, or success story pages on the current site. For enterprise GRC software — where decisions involve multiple stakeholders, long sales cycles, and meaningful compliance risk — peer validation is the single most influential trust signal. Competitors AuditBoard and MetricStream lead with detailed case studies. The current site has none.
13
RegTech100 2026 Recognition Not Visible in Key Locations
Jethur's inclusion in the RegTech100 2026 list is a significant, independently verified credibility signal. It is not displayed in the hero section, the homepage trust area, or the navigation. It appears in a location that most visitors will not see. This is a high-value signal being wasted on the current site.
14
No Client Logos or Partner Logos on Homepage
The homepage does not display any client logos, partner logos, or "trusted by" messaging. Even a single recognised logo from a client or integration partner would materially increase perceived legitimacy. The current homepage offers no social proof of this kind.
15
Blog Is Sparse and Infrequently Updated
The blog has limited content and shows infrequent updates. For a GRC platform, a blog is both an SEO asset and a trust signal — it signals that the company has active expertise in the compliance space. The current blog conveys neither.
16
Homepage Returns 403 Forbidden to Direct HTTP Requests
jethur.com returns a 403 Forbidden error to direct HTTP requests. The implication is that search engine crawlers — Googlebot, Bingbot — may be receiving the same 403 response, preventing the homepage from being indexed. A site that cannot be crawled cannot rank in search.
17
Site Indexability at Risk for Some Search Engines
As a consequence of the 403 issue above, the homepage's presence in search results is uncertain. Jethur has invested in 7+ compliance framework pages (ISO 27001, SOC 2, NCA ECC, etc.) that carry organic search value — if the site's crawlability is compromised, this content may not be reaching the compliance professionals searching for it.
18
No Structured Data / Schema Markup on Any Page
No schema markup (JSON-LD or microdata) was detected on any page of the current site. Structured data helps search engines categorise and display content as rich results. For a SaaS product, the absence of schema on product pages and the blog means the site is leaving search visibility potential unused.
19
Meta Descriptions Missing or Generic on Key Pages
Multiple primary pages — including /products and /jethur-ai — either have no meta description or use generic, non-descriptive text. Meta descriptions are the first copy a prospect reads in a search result. The current site is not using this space to communicate a value proposition.
20
Homepage Hero Lacks Clear Visual Hierarchy
The homepage hero does not guide the eye in a clear order. The headline, sub-headline, and CTA are insufficiently differentiated in size and weight, making it difficult for a first-time visitor to identify what Jethur is, who it is for, and what to do — within the 5-second window that most homepage visitors give a site before forming a first impression.
21
Compliance Pages Are Isolated — No Links to Product
The 7 compliance framework pages (ISO 27001, SOC 2, NCA ECC, etc.) function as standalone content with no internal links to product pages. A visitor arriving via search on one of these pages — likely a high-intent compliance professional — reads the content and hits a dead end with no path toward the product.
22
Contact and Demo Forms Are Visually Off-Brand
Both the Contact form and Demo form use plain, unstyled input fields with thin bottom-only borders and a generic blue "Send request" button that does not match Jethur's brand colours (dark green, orange). Against a site with bold typography and strong visual identity, these bare forms feel like placeholder elements. The blue button in particular is a default WordPress/WPForms colour and creates an inconsistency that erodes trust at the exact moment a buyer is deciding whether to submit their details.
23
Footer Is Misaligned and Disorganised
The footer has several layout problems: (1) Social media icons appear twice — once beside the logo and again beside the address in the bottom section, with no reason for the duplication. (2) The three navigation columns (Jethur, Regions, Frameworks) are spaced with large uneven gaps, making the footer feel unstructured. (3) The bottom bar splits address, certifications, and contact across three disconnected areas that are not vertically aligned. (4) The certification badges sit in the middle of the bottom bar with no label or context above them at that level. For enterprise buyers who scroll to the footer to find contact details or legal links, this disorganisation reduces confidence.
24
About Us Page Has No Named People or Leadership Team
The About Us page contains no names, no photos, no roles, and no bios. Enterprise GRC buyers always research who is behind the company before engaging — they need to know the founders are credible, that there is relevant experience in compliance and risk, and that there is a real team behind the platform. Without any visible leadership, the company appears anonymous. Competitors in the GRC space prominently feature founder credentials, advisory boards, and team pages. This is a direct trust barrier at the point where buyers are trying to decide whether to request a demo.
25
"Spanning the Globe" Section Is Empty — Shows Only a World Map
The "Spanning the Globe" section on the About page shows a world map with no supporting content — no office locations marked, no regional presence listed, no client countries highlighted, and no accompanying text explaining what the global presence actually means. The heading promises international reach but the section delivers nothing. For a buyer trying to assess whether Jethur can support their region, this section actively creates doubt rather than building confidence. Either populate it with real data (countries served, offices, regulatory frameworks supported per region) or remove the section entirely.
26
Solutions Portfolio Listed With No Context or Description
The About page lists Jethur's solution areas (RootX, AI, etc.) as headings only, with no description of what each solves, who it is for, or how it differs from alternatives. A buyer who lands on the About page to understand the company's scope will leave without understanding the product portfolio. Each solution should have at minimum a one-sentence description and a link to the relevant product page. As currently structured, the section reads as a placeholder rather than a credibility signal.
27
No Company History, Founding Year, or Mission Statement
The About page contains no information about when the company was founded, what prompted its creation, or what the company's long-term mission is. For enterprise buyers conducting vendor due diligence, the absence of a founding story raises questions about the company's maturity and stability. A short founding narrative — even two or three sentences — explaining why Jethur was built, when it launched, and what problem it set out to solve would significantly increase buyer confidence and differentiate Jethur from newer, less credible entrants in the GRC market.
28
Review Platform Badges Shown Without Ratings or Scores
The homepage displays Trustpilot, G2, Capterra, and similar review platform logos near the bottom of the page, but no actual scores, star ratings, or review counts are shown alongside them. Showing the badge without the number is the weakest possible use of social proof — it signals that the company is aware of these platforms but has nothing specific to show. Enterprise buyers who see these badges will click through to verify. If ratings are strong, display them prominently with the score (e.g., "4.7/5 on G2 — 120+ reviews"). If ratings are thin or absent, remove the badges entirely rather than inviting a click that leads nowhere credible.
29
Solutions Section Uses Decorative Progress Bars With No Meaning
In the "Explore Our Solutions" section, each solution (ERC, ERM, Audit Management, etc.) is accompanied by a coloured horizontal bar. These bars have no label, no scale, no percentage, and no explanation of what they measure. They appear to be decorative elements styled to look like data. For enterprise GRC buyers — who are evaluating software for compliance and risk management — presenting fake data visualisations is a credibility problem. Buyers who notice that the bars carry no meaning will question whether the site's other claims are equally hollow. Remove the bars or replace them with actual, labelled metrics (e.g., implementation time, number of supported frameworks, customer count per module).
30
"Pioneering Force" Section Makes Unsubstantiated Claims
The homepage section titled "Pioneering Force in the Technology Sector" lists five claims — Decades of Expertise, Dynamic Team, Commitment to Quality, Global Experience, Revolutionary Solutions — with no supporting numbers, dates, or evidence. "Decades" implies 20+ years of history but no founding year is provided anywhere on the site. "Dynamic Team" gives no headcount. "Global Experience" is not backed by a country or client list. "Revolutionary Solutions" is subjective marketing language with no differentiator. Each of these claims has the potential to be a credibility asset if backed with a real number or fact. As currently written, they read as placeholder copy and reduce rather than build trust with sceptical enterprise buyers.
